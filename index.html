How to Set a Realistic Fitness Goal

Ever gone in to a gym to check it out and the first question that sales staff asks you is: “What are your goals?” the first time I went into a gym and was asked this question, I had a very broad “get more fit” answer. Not a very realistic fitness goal that I could track and adjust as needed.

Setting realistic fitness goals sounds easy. So easy, that many people end up setting unrealistic fitness goals leading themselves to failure.

Whether starting, or continuing, a fitness program, it’s important to set realistic fitness goals that are specific and measurable. Goals are your road map by which you will create a detailed plan to better health and a stronger you. Here’s a simple truism about goals: Without a goal, how will you know where you are going, how to get there and when you have reached your end desire?

Goals have levels of specificity. There are higher level, general goals and more detailed, specific goals. In this article, we will talk about general and specific goals that are realistic and will help you improve your health and fitness.

The General Goal

Start out by creating broad, high level general fitness and health goals. General goals provide a broad outline of what you are trying to achieve. However, they do NOT provide the “how” of getting there. That is reserved for specific goals.

For example: a high level goal might be “I want to get stronger.” When setting this goal, you are broadly saying this is what you want, but it does not help you to understand or measure the future desired fitness level. It is too broad and much too overwhelming for that sort of measurement.

To be able to create a metric against which you can measure your goal achievement, you need to refine your general goals into smaller, more specific targets so that you can identify specific steps to reach them.

Specific Goals

Specific fitness and health goals are realistic, actionable and measurable ways to improve your fitness level and achieve your overall general goal. Put another way, specific goals are an accumulation of realistic actions that you take in small bites to accomplish a big picture, general goal.

An example of a specific goal is: I want to be able to do 100 sit ups per exercise session at some specific point in time.

Once you have a realistic specific goal, you can start to set up your plan to achieve that goal. This gives you the steps to work towards a target.

There are Several Key Factors that Go into Setting a Realistic, Specific Goal

Realistic and Attainable Goals

Ask yourself, have I set realistic and attainable goals?

If you set a goal so high that you will not be able to achieve it, you will set yourself up for failure.

For example: 100 situps might sound realistic and is specific. But, instead of working toward doing 100 sit ups all at once, set 100 as your general sit up goal and first target doing 25, then 50, then 75 and finally 100 to achieve your general goal. Bites of 25 sit ups are both specific and most importantly realistic on your way to 100 a session.

With each 25, you’ll feel that sense of achievement reaching important milestones that keep you motivated and focused.

Measure Your Progress

To see your progress, record how many sit ups you do each session. This way, you will get a sense of your overall fitness level and clearly see how and when you improve to motivate yourself to reach your goal.

Pick a Time Frame to Accomplish Your General and Specific Goals

Setting a time frame on each goal type will also help to keep you motivated and working toward those goals. This will indicate your pace of progress. If you feel it’s too slow, you may need to adjust your milestones.

Keep Your Goals Flexible

Goals are meant to not only be accomplished, but also to be fine-tuned. Goal tuning means that you may need to change the time frame, the milestones or the general end goal to make sure you can accomplish it.

Remember this process will help show you what you can attain and how to attain it. Biting off more than you can chew impedes success. You may even find that you set the bar too low and that you can do more than you originally thought you could. This, too, is a valid measure of your progress.

Flexible goals are easier to refine and accomplish. You are looking for progress, not perfection.

Bottom Line

When you are specific about what you want, setting measurable targets, you are more likely to achieve what you are after. Higher level, general end goals to improve your fitness are best attained by setting and accomplishing short term specific fitness goals.

Establishing specific goals will help you to define the steps you need accomplish them. Measuring and tracking your goals will show you how much progress you are making along the way. Adjusting your goals where needed will keep you motivated and keep your goals attainable and realistic.
